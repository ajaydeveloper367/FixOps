Architecture Challenges — My Questions for You

Based on reading both codebases and your requirements. These are genuine decision points — your answers change what to build first.

1. MongoDB is your most critical gap — and IncidentFox has zero of it Decision point

Your primary alert scenario is "pdata failed messages" — a MongoDB collection. HolmesGPT has TWO native Mongo paths (wire protocol + Atlas REST). IncidentFox has none and it's not on a visible roadmap in the codebase.

Is building a Mongo skill from scratch in IncidentFox actually cheaper than starting with HolmesGPT which already has it and adding the multi-agent layer on top?

Mongo investigation is not optional for your use case — it's the first check in your most common alert flow. This gap alone is worth reconsidering the IncidentFox-first assumption.

2. Rules-first routing: is it worth building custom in Phase 1? Decision point

IncidentFox's orchestrator routing is config-driven at the top level (channel → team → agent). For Phase 1, this may be "good enough" — your ambiguous data-entity alerts (collection → graph → service) are the edge case, not the common case. Most alerts have a pod name, service name, or DAG name.

Can Phase 1 use config-driven routing and add the rules-based entity resolver (collection → dataflow graph → service) only in Phase 2 once you see real routing failures in production?

Building a full routing engine upfront is expensive. Most alerts will have enough context to route without the graph. Start simple, add complexity where you observe failures.

3. Hybrid approach: IncidentFox framework + HolmesGPT tools as MCP servers Decision point

IncidentFox already supports MCP servers (local/claude_code_pack MCP server exists in the repo). HolmesGPT's toolsets — Mongo, Tempo, Kafka, Confluence — are clean Python classes that can be wrapped as MCP servers in 1-2 days each.

Is the hybrid (IncidentFox framework + HolmesGPT tools via MCP) worth the extra complexity, or is it simpler to just build missing tools as IncidentFox skills directly?

HolmesGPT's Mongo and Tempo toolsets are ~300 lines each, well-tested, production-grade. Wrapping them as MCP takes a day. Writing them fresh in IncidentFox skill format takes longer and you'd be duplicating proven code.

4. LLM cost at your scale — have you estimated this? Decision point

DAGs × multiple services × Kafka topics × Mongo collections = potentially hundreds of alerts per day. Each investigation = controller call + 2–4 worker calls + RCA summary. At GPT-4o/Claude Sonnet prices, this adds up fast.

What is your expected alert volume per day in production? And what is your monthly LLM cost tolerance? Have you modelled this?

Small model for routing/extraction + medium for worker RCA + large only for hard multi-source cases. This model tiering is essential, not optional, at your scale. Both projects let you configure per-agent model — use it.

5. Inventory bootstrap: who maintains it as DAG count grows? Decision point

Your design needs a thin manual inventory (service → runtime, cluster, owner, repo) as the routing source. For 20–30 critical services this is easy. As DAG count and collection count grow, even "thin" becomes a maintenance burden without auto-sync.

Can you commit to auto-syncing inventory from your EKS service labels, Airflow DAG metadata (owner, tags), and Mongo Atlas tags from the start? Or will this be manually maintained YAML?

Auto-sync from existing metadata is Phase 4 work. Starting with manual YAML for your top 30 critical services is realistic for Phase 1–2 and buys you time to build the auto-extraction pipeline.
